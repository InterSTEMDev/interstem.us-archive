Dexamethasone: The Common Drug's Role in Minimizing Coronavirus Deaths Annabel Tiong. 6/24/2020

Studies from the WHO show that coronavirus cases are slowly increasing. (Irfan Khan / Los Angeles Times)


A common steroid known as Dexamethasone reduced deaths in patients with severe cases of COVID-19 recently in Britain, the New York Times reports. According to researchers, around 5,000 deaths could have been prevented as the drug protects the tissues by reducing inflammation.

The nationwide Recovery Trial, which aims to identify potential treatments for COVID-19, announced that "a low-dose regimen of dexamethasone for 10 days was found to reduce the risk of death by a third among hospitalized patients requiring ventilation in the trial," CNN reports.

According to Mayo Clinic, Dexamethasone is a corticosteroid that has been used since the 1960s to treat conditions such as asthma, skin conditions, adrenal problems, arthritis, and even severe allergies. It is available on the shelf as a relatively inexpensive product, and has been listed on the World Health Organization (WHO) Model List of Essential Medicines since 1977 in multiple formulations.

"Dexamethasone is the first drug to be shown to improve survival in COVID-19. This is an extremely welcome result," Peter Horby, professor of Emerging Infectious Diseases in the Nuffield Department of Medicine, University of Oxford told CBS News.

Many doctors and renowned healthcare specialists are also calling this discovery a "major breakthrough."

WHO Director-General, Dr. Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus, even congratulated the University of Oxford and hospitals in the UK on the contributions to the findings. However, the World Health Organization clarifies that the benefit was only seen in patients with life-threatening cases of COVID-19. Their latest news release details how they will "coordinate a meta-analysis to increase...overall understanding of [the] intervention."

WHO clinical guidance will then be updated to reflect how and when the drug should be used in COVID-19.

Nevertheless, others are still skeptical about the validity of its effects. "After all the retractions and walk backs, it is unacceptable to tout study results by press release without releasing the paper," Dr. Atul Gawande, surgeon and author, said in a tweet.

Currently, there is no vaccine against coronavirus and remdesivir is the only antiviral drug proven to be effective.

"We hope the data on which these results are based will be published as soon as possible so that doctors can confidently put the treatment into practice," Robin Ferner, honorary professor of clinical pharmacology at the University of Birmingham told CNN.
